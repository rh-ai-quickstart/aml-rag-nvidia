# Parasol Financial Suspicious Activity Reporting Drafting Standards

Parasol Financial

## Purpose

This document defines internal drafting expectations for case summaries prepared in support of suspicious activity reporting decisions. The purpose is to ensure reports are factual, clear, and useful to reviewers and downstream stakeholders.

## Drafting Principles

Case narratives must be accurate, objective, concise, and evidence-based. They should explain why the activity is unusual, what investigative steps were taken, and why the bank concluded the activity warrants reporting or further escalation. Writers must avoid speculation, inflammatory language, and unsupported assumptions about customer intent.

## Required Narrative Elements

A strong case summary identifies the customer and relevant account relationship, describes the activity under review, explains why the activity is unusual or suspicious, summarizes the supporting evidence, notes any customer explanation if available, and states the final rationale for escalation or reporting.

## Writing Style

Use plain, factual language. Prefer specific transaction descriptions over vague statements. For example, a strong sentence describes a pattern of rapid incoming wires followed by outgoing transfers to unrelated counterparties over a defined period. A weak sentence says only that activity appeared suspicious.

## Source Citation in Internal Drafts

Internal draft summaries should reference the evidence reviewed, such as transaction history, account opening documents, prior case history, internal policy guidance, and any approved external screening results. They should not include information taken from unapproved or informal sources.

## What to Avoid

Do not state that a customer committed a crime. Do not assign motive unless supported by approved evidence and legal review. Do not include irrelevant background information. Do not overstate certainty when facts are incomplete. Do not copy large blocks of system-generated text without editing for clarity.

## Standard Draft Structure

The opening paragraph should identify the subject, products involved, and review period. The middle section should describe the suspicious pattern and investigative findings. The final section should explain the disposition, escalation reason, or recommendation.

## Example Internal Template Language

During the review period, the customer’s account activity showed a material increase in cross-border wires inconsistent with the stated business profile captured at onboarding. Funds received from multiple unrelated counterparties were transferred out within short timeframes, often to destinations outside the customer’s normal operating pattern. Review of available documentation did not establish a clear business purpose for the activity.

## Review Requirements

All draft case summaries intended for SAR consideration must be reviewed by a senior investigator or SAR governance reviewer before final submission into downstream reporting workflows.
