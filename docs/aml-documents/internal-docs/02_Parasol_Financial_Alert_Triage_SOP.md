# Parasol Financial Alert Triage Standard Operating Procedure

Parasol Financial

## Purpose

This procedure defines how AML alerts are prioritized before full investigation. The goal is to route higher-risk activity to investigators quickly while ensuring lower-risk alerts are reviewed consistently.

## Triage Principles

Triage is not a final investigative decision. It is an initial risk-based assessment used to prioritize work. Analysts performing triage should consider the nature of the alert, customer risk, transaction characteristics, geography, product type, and any prior compliance history.

## Alert Priority Levels

Priority 1 – Critical: Immediate review required. These alerts involve potential sanctions overlap, large unexplained cross-border movement, suspected structuring tied to known high-risk factors, apparent use of multiple related accounts for layering, or other activity with a credible nexus to serious financial crime.

Priority 2 – High: Same-day or next-business-day review. These alerts involve material unexplained activity, repeated unusual transactions, significant change from customer baseline, or high-risk customer categories.

Priority 3 – Moderate: Standard review queue. These alerts involve moderate deviations from expected behavior without immediate signs of severe escalation.

Priority 4 – Low: Lower-risk review. These alerts involve small-value activity, isolated anomalies, or patterns that may be readily explainable based on available account information.

## Triage Factors

The following factors increase priority: high-value or rapid movement of funds, new accounts showing unusual velocity, significant cash activity inconsistent with profile, cross-border wires with unclear purpose, use of multiple beneficiaries or originators without clear business rationale, negative history in prior cases, changes in customer ownership or control, and links to high-risk industries or high-risk jurisdictions as defined by internal policy.

The following factors may lower priority: activity consistent with well-documented expected behavior, long-tenured customers with stable and explainable transaction patterns, prior similar alerts that were reasonably resolved with current supporting evidence, and low materiality with no meaningful compounding factors.

## Triage Workflow

The analyst reviews the alert metadata, customer type, products involved, recent transaction summary, prior alert count, customer risk rating, and any existing comments. The analyst then assigns a priority and records a short rationale. If an immediate sanctions, fraud, or legal risk is evident, the case should bypass standard triage and be referred according to the Escalation Matrix.

## Required Triage Note

Every triaged alert must include a note stating what triggered the alert, key risk factors observed, customer risk tier, whether similar alerts exist, and why the case was assigned its priority level.

## Reassignment and Queue Management

Managers may reassign cases when new information changes risk. Repeated moderate alerts involving the same customer should be reviewed together where possible to avoid fragmented decision-making.

## Quality Expectations

Triage decisions must be explainable and consistent. Analysts should avoid using generic notes such as “high risk” without identifying what specifically created the risk signal.
